Why is a linear transformation a (1,1)(1,1) tensor?

Wikipedia says that a linear transformation is a (1,1)(1,1) tensor. Is this restricting it to transformations from VV to VV or is a transformation from VV to WW also a (1,1)(1,1) tensor? (where VV and WW are both vector spaces). I think it must be the first case since it also states that a linear functional is a (0,1)(0,1) tensor and this is a transformation from VV to RR. If it is the second case, could you please explain why linear transformations are (1,1)(1,1) tensors.

It's very common in tensor analysis to associate endomorphisms on a vector space with (1,1) tensors. Namely because there exists an isomorphism between the two sets.

Define E(V)E(V) to be the set of endomorphisms on VV.

Let A∈E(V)A∈E(V) and define the map Θ:E(V)→T11(V)Θ:E(V)→T11(V) by

(ΘA)(ω,X)=ω(AX).(ΘA)(ω,X)=ω(AX).
We show that ΘΘ is an isomorphism of vector spaces. Let {ei}{ei} be a basis for VV and let {εi}{εi} be the corresponding dual basis. First, we note ΘΘ is linear by the linearity of ωω. To show injectivity, suppose ΘA=ΘBΘA=ΘB for some A,B∈E(V)A,B∈E(V) and let X∈VX∈V, ω∈V∗ω∈V∗ be arbitrary. Then
(ΘA)(ω,X)⟺ω(AX−BX)=(ΘB)(ω,X)=0.(ΘA)(ω,X)=(ΘB)(ω,X)⟺ω(AX−BX)=0.
Since XX and ωω were arbitrary, it follows that
AX⟺A=BX=B.AX=BX⟺A=B.
To show surjectivity, suppose f∈T11f∈T11 has coordinate representation fjiεi⊗ejfijεi⊗ej. We wish to find A∈E(V)A∈E(V) such that ΘA=fΘA=f. We simply choose A∈E(V)A∈E(V) such that AA has the matrix representation (fji)(fij). If we write the representation of our vector XX and covector ωω as
Xω=Xiei=ωiεi,X=Xieiω=ωiεi,
we have
(ΘA)(ω,X)=ω(AX)=ωkεk(fjiXiej)=fjiXiωkεk(ej)=fjiXiωkδkj=fkiXiωk.(ΘA)(ω,X)=ω(AX)=ωkεk(fijXiej)=fijXiωkεk(ej)=fijXiωkδjk=fikXiωk.
However we see
f(ω,X)=f(ωkεk,Xiei)=ωkXif(εk,ei)=fkiXiωk.f(ω,X)=f(ωkεk,Xiei)=ωkXif(εk,ei)=fikXiωk.
Since XX and ωω were arbitrary, it follows that ΘA=fΘA=f. Thus, ΘΘ is linear and bijective, hence an isomorphism.

Let T:V↦WT:V↦W. Then define τ:V×W∗↦Kτ:V×W∗↦K such that, for a∈Va∈V and α∈W∗α∈W∗, we have

τ(a,α)=(α∘T)(a)τ(a,α)=(α∘T)(a)

Note that it's typical to define tensor to mean a multilinear map that is a function of vectors only in the same vector space, or of covectors in the associated dual space, or some combination of the two. So, we could identify a linear operator T:V↦VT:V↦V with a (1,1)(1,1) tensor τ:V×V∗↦Kτ:V×V∗↦K, but in the case that VV and WW are distinct vector spaces, these would just be some construction of multilinear maps, not tensors.

To summarize as an answer what I wrote in various comments above: first beware that autors differ in their definition of tensor, even when using the same approach, i.e. using the tensor product in this case.

For some authors a tensor is defined only as ...

T∈V⊗⋯⊗Vn copies⊗V∗⊗⋯⊗V∗m copiesT∈V⊗⋯⊗V⏟n copies⊗V∗⊗⋯⊗V∗⏟m copies

From which it makes sense to speak of a type-(n,m)(n,m) tensor.

For others, a tensor is any...

T∈V1⊗⋯⊗VdT∈V1⊗⋯⊗Vd

where V1,…,VdV1,…,Vd can be different vector spaces, however all must be over the same scalar field. And with this latter definition one can speak of an order-dd tensor. A type-(n,m)(n,m) tensor [in the former sense] is a tensor of order d=n+md=n+m in the latter sense, but second definition is broader for it does not restrict us to a single vector space. In particular, a second-order tensor is an element of V⊗WV⊗W where VV and WW may be two different vector spaces. Type-(1,1) tensors are tensors of second order, but the converse of this statement doesn't make sense. (N.B.: I've updated Wikipedia to reflect these different definitions.)

As for your 2nd question, endomorphisms (linear maps) from a vector space to itself are (isomorphic with) type-(1,1) tensors (detailed proof given here by beedge89), but if you consider homomorphisms (linear maps) between different vector spaces VV and WW, i.e. Hom(V,W)Hom(V,W), these are isomorphic with only a certain class of order-2 tensors, namely with V∗⊗WV∗⊗W. If we let (ϕ,w)∈V∗×W(ϕ,w)∈V∗×W, then the correspondence is given by ϕ⊗w↔Fϕ,wϕ⊗w↔Fϕ,w, where the latter is a (linear) map defined as Fϕ,w(v)=ϕ(v)wFϕ,w(v)=ϕ(v)w. (Remember that covectors are themselves maps from vectors to scalars, so the formula for FF makes sense as it's a product of the scalar ϕ(v)ϕ(v) with the vector ww). A detailed proof of the fact that this is an isomorphism is given in Yokonuma (pp. 18-19). Apologies for not including it here.

As you may expect, the result for type-(1,1) tensors also follows as a corollary of this, i.e. Hom(V,V)Hom(V,V) is isomorphic with V∗⊗VV∗⊗V (and with V⊗V∗V⊗V∗ by commutativity of the tensor product, which is also understood in the sense of an isomorphism between V⊗WV⊗W and W⊗VW⊗V for any vector spaces VV and WW).

And one important caveat here: this is an isomorphism only for finite-dimensional vector spaces. (The introduction of Yokonuma's book actually says to assume all vector spaces in the book are finite-dimensional unless stated otherwise.) If both VV and WW are infinite-dimensional, then it turns out V∗⊗WV∗⊗W is only a proper subspace of Hom(V,W)Hom(V,W), namely it is the subspace of linear transformation of finite rank.

And to tie this in with bilinear (and in general with multi-linear) maps: there's also a one-one correspondence between bilinear maps f:V×W→Uf:V×W→U and linearlinear maps g:V⊗W→Ug:V⊗W→U. (For a proof see for instance http://www.landsburg.com/algebra.pdf) That's why second-order tensors are basically said to be just bilinear maps, and in general why d-order tensors are said to be just multi-linear maps.
